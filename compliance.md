# Compliance and Regulatory

## Privacy Regulations

### Delaware Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA)
- **Scope** — Delaware residents’ personal data, applies to entities doing business in Delaware or targeting Delaware residents that meet statutory thresholds
- **Key requirements** — Privacy notice, data minimization, reasonable safeguards, consent for sensitive data, data protection assessments for higher risk processing, opt out support for targeted advertising and sale of personal data
- **Rights** — Access, correct, delete, obtain a portable copy, opt out of sale, targeted advertising, and certain profiling
- **Penalties** — Enforced by Delaware DOJ, civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation, no private right of action

### Wyoming Data Breach Notification Statute
- **Scope** — Individuals and commercial entities conducting business in Wyoming that own, license, or maintain covered information about Wyoming residents
- **Key requirements** — Notify affected individuals in the most expedient time possible without unreasonable delay after determination of misuse, with required notice content and limited safe harbors and exceptions
- **Rights** — Breach notification when covered information is reasonably believed to be misused, subject to harm threshold and law enforcement delay rules
- **Penalties** — Civil penalties, no private right of action

### Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA, Singapore)
- **Scope** — Organizations that collect, use, disclose, or otherwise handle personal data in Singapore, subject to statutory exclusions
- **Key requirements** — Consent and purpose limitation, DPO, security safeguards, breach notification, cross-border transfer protections, Do Not Call registry framework
- **Rights** — Access and correction, plus related controls such as withdrawal of consent in applicable contexts
- **Penalties** — Up to 10% of annual turnover in Singapore for organizations with annual turnover in Singapore exceeding S$10M, otherwise up to S$1M

### Personal Data Protection Law (PDP Law, Indonesia, Law No. 27 of 2022)
- **Scope** — Processing of personal data by controllers and processors in Indonesia
- **Key requirements** — Lawful basis framework, security measures, breach notification, cross-border transfer conditions, DPIA and DPO requirements in certain higher risk contexts
- **Rights** — Access, correction, deletion, withdrawal of consent in relevant cases
- **Penalties** — Administrative sanctions including fines up to 2% of annual income or revenue, plus potential criminal sanctions for certain unlawful acts

### Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA, Thailand, B.E. 2562)
- **Scope** — Personal data of individuals in Thailand processed by controllers and processors, including relevant cross-border scenarios
- **Key requirements** — Notice and lawful basis, consent management, security safeguards, mechanisms to handle data subject requests, DPO in certain cases, cross-border transfer controls
- **Rights** — Access, withdraw consent, rectify, delete, restrict or object, data portability, lodge complaint
- **Penalties** — Administrative fines up to THB 5,000,000, plus civil and criminal exposure depending on the violation

### Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance (PDPO, Hong Kong)
- **Scope** — Personal data handled by a data user in or from Hong Kong, governed by the Data Protection Principles and specific statutory provisions
- **Key requirements** — Comply with Data Protection Principles, support data access and correction processes, comply with direct marketing consent and opt out rules, follow enforcement notices where issued
- **Rights** — Data access and data correction, plus opt out controls for direct marketing
- **Penalties** — Criminal penalties vary by offence, including fines up to HK$1,000,000 and up to 5 years imprisonment for certain offences, and penalties for contravening enforcement notices

### Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL, China)
- **Scope** — Personal information of natural persons in China, including extraterritorial application in certain circumstances
- **Key requirements** — Consent and lawful bases, enhanced rules for sensitive personal information, cross-border transfer mechanisms and assessments in some cases, security measures and impact assessments for higher risk processing
- **Rights** — Access and copy, correct, delete, withdraw consent, and other statutory controls over processing
- **Penalties** — For serious violations, fines up to RMB 50 million or 5% of the previous year’s revenue, plus potential business suspension and other measures

## Industry Compliance

### SOC 2
- **Type 1** — Point-in-time control design
- **Type 2** — Operating effectiveness over period (6-12 months)
- **Trust principles** — Security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, privacy
- **Timeline** — 3-6 months readiness, 3-12 months audit period

### HIPAA (Healthcare)
- **Covered entities** — Healthcare providers, plans, clearinghouses
- **Business associates** — BAA required for all vendors handling PHI
- **Safeguards** — Administrative, physical, technical
- **Breach notification** — 60 days to HHS, individuals

### PCI DSS (Payment Cards)
- **Levels** — Based on transaction volume
- **SAQ vs ROC** — Self-assessment vs external audit
- **Key controls** — Network segmentation, encryption, access control
- **Annual validation** — Plus quarterly scans for some levels

### Financial Services
- **SOX** — Internal controls for public companies
- **GLBA** — Consumer financial privacy
- **AML/KYC** — Customer identification, suspicious activity reporting

## Employment and International Corporate Structures

### Executive Employment and Officer Compensation
- **Scope** — How a CEO or executive is engaged and compensated across a Delaware or Wyoming TopCo and operating entities in Singapore, Indonesia, Thailand, Hong Kong, and China
- **Key requirements** — Written engagement terms, clear role definition, board approvals for officer and director compensation, local payroll registration where required, proper reporting of employment income and benefits
- **Rights** — Statutory employment protections depend on the jurisdiction and whether the person is treated as an employee, director, or independent contractor
- **Penalties** — Misclassification exposure, payroll tax and withholding penalties, immigration violations, and corporate law governance issues if approvals and disclosures are not followed

### Cross Border Payroll, Withholding, and Social Security
- **Scope** — Compensation paid for work physically performed in Singapore, Indonesia, Thailand, Hong Kong, or China can create local tax and withholding obligations even if the employer is a Delaware entity
- **Key requirements** — Identify where services are performed, determine tax residence and source rules, register payroll withholding where required, handle employer reporting, and apply treaty relief only with proper documentation where applicable
- **Rights** — Employees and directors generally have rights to payslips, statutory contributions where applicable, and proper tax documentation
- **Penalties** — Employer withholding liabilities, late filing penalties, interest, and potential inability to deduct compensation as a business expense in the paying entity

### Service Company and Management Fee Arrangements
- **Scope** — Getting paid through an entity for services instead of a traditional salary, such as a CEO providing services via a consulting company to a Delaware TopCo or Singapore subsidiary
- **Key requirements** — Commercial rationale, arm’s length pricing, written service agreements, evidence of deliverables, proper invoicing, transfer pricing documentation where applicable, and consistency between legal form and operational reality
- **Rights** — The individual’s rights shift based on classification, with reduced statutory employment protections if treated as a contractor in some jurisdictions
- **Penalties** — Recharacterization risk where tax authorities treat fees as employment income, denial of deductions for excessive or non business payments, and anti avoidance challenges if arrangements lack substance

### Permanent Establishment and Corporate Tax Presence Risk
- **Scope** — A Delaware TopCo can create taxable presence in another country if executive decision making or revenue generating activity is effectively conducted there
- **Key requirements** — Document where central management and control occurs, manage signing authority and contract execution location, align governance with operational footprints, and evaluate whether local subsidiaries or branches trigger additional obligations
- **Rights** — Not applicable
- **Penalties** — Corporate tax exposure, back taxes, penalties, and additional filing obligations in the country where a taxable presence is found

### Example Structure and Tax Implications
- **Scope** — CEO of a Delaware TopCo, day to day operations via a Singapore subsidiary, and compensation structured as service fees paid to a CEO owned entity rather than a salary
- **Key requirements** — Confirm whether the CEO is effectively an employee of the TopCo or subsidiary based on control and integration, assess local payroll and employment reporting where the CEO is physically working, validate service company substance and arm’s length fees, confirm cross border withholding obligations on management fees and director remuneration where applicable
- **Rights** — The CEO may still be treated as an employee for tax and labor purposes in some jurisdictions regardless of invoicing form, depending on control, exclusivity, and integration factors
- **Penalties** — Potential reclassification of service fees into salary or director remuneration, payroll withholding assessments, transfer pricing adjustments, and compliance issues if immigration and work authorization do not match the engagement model

## Compliance Program Components

### Foundation
1. **Risk assessment** — Annual, documented, prioritized
2. **Policies and procedures** — Written, accessible, version controlled
3. **Training** — Role-based, annual refresh, tracked completion
4. **Monitoring** — Controls testing, exception tracking

### Ongoing Operations
1. **Vendor management** — Due diligence, contractual requirements, monitoring
2. **Incident response** — Documented plan, tested annually, contact lists current
3. **Audit support** — Evidence collection, finding remediation, trend analysis
4. **Regulatory monitoring** — New laws, enforcement actions, industry guidance
